§ 01

Partial Derivatives

When a function depends on many variables, the partial derivative asks a simple question — "if I wiggle just one variable, holding all others still, how does the output change?"

Formal Definition

For a function f(x₁, x₂, …, xₙ), the partial derivative with respect to xᵢ measures the rate of change of f when only xᵢ changes:

Definition ∂f/∂xᵢ = limh→0 [ f(x₁, …, xᵢ + h, …, xₙ) − f(x₁, …, xᵢ, …, xₙ) ] / h

The other variables are treated as constants. This is the simplest possible generalization of the single-variable derivative.

→ Intuition

Imagine standing on a mountain (the function surface). The partial derivative ∂f/∂x is the slope you'd feel if you could only walk strictly east-west. ∂f/∂y is the slope strictly north-south.

Worked Example

Let f(x, y) = x²y + 3y² + xy. Then:

∂f/∂x = 2xy + y (treat y as constant)
∂f/∂y = x² + 6y + x (treat x as constant)

Diagram

Contour map of f(x, y). Partial derivatives at P measure slope along the x-axis (red) and y-axis (blue) directions.

How this shows up in AI

Backpropagation IS partial derivatives. A neural network's loss L is a function of millions of weights w₁, w₂, …, wₙ. To update each weight, we need ∂L/∂wᵢ — how the loss changes if we tweak just that one weight.

Frameworks like PyTorch compute this for every parameter in your model, on every batch, billions of times during training. The chain rule applied recursively over a computation graph is just partial derivatives, all the way down.

PyTorch implementation

import torch # Two parameters — like a tiny 2-weight model x = torch.tensor(2.0, requires_grad=True) y = torch.tensor(3.0, requires_grad=True) f = x**2 * y + 3*y**2 + x*y f.backward() print(x.grad) # ∂f/∂x = 2xy + y = 12 + 3 = 15 print(y.grad) # ∂f/∂y = x² + 6y + x = 4 + 18 + 2 = 24
§ 02

The Gradient Vector

If partial derivatives are the individual ingredients, the gradient is the recipe — a single vector that bundles them all together and points in the direction of steepest ascent.

Formal Definition

The gradient of f: ℝⁿ → ℝ is the column vector of all its partial derivatives:

Gradient ∇f(x) = [ ∂f/∂x₁, ∂f/∂x₂, …, ∂f/∂xₙ ]ᵀ

Two Critical Properties

  1. Direction: ∇f points in the direction of steepest ascent at point x. Move along it, f increases fastest. Move opposite to it (−∇f), f decreases fastest.
  2. Magnitude: ‖∇f‖ tells you how steep that ascent is. Where ∇f = 0, you've found a critical point (min, max, or saddle).

The gradient ∇f points uphill. Descent moves in the negative gradient direction toward the minimum.

"The gradient is the compass needle of optimization — and ML is, fundamentally, optimization."

How this shows up in AI

Gradient descent is the heartbeat of deep learning. Every optimizer — SGD, Adam, RMSProp, AdamW — starts from the same idea:

wt+1 = wt − η · ∇L(wt)

Where η is the learning rate and ∇L is the gradient of loss with respect to all parameters. Adam and friends just add momentum, adaptive scaling, and bias correction on top of this skeleton.

When people say "the model trained for 100,000 steps," they mean "the parameters were nudged in the −∇L direction 100,000 times."

§ 03

Directional Derivatives

Partials only let you move along axes. Directional derivatives generalize this — they ask, "what's the slope if I walk in any direction?"

Formal Definition

For a unit vector u, the directional derivative of f at point x in direction u is:

Directional Derivative Duf(x) = ∇f(x) · u (dot product)
        = ‖∇f‖ · ‖u‖ · cos(θ)
        = ‖∇f‖ · cos(θ) (since ‖u‖ = 1)

where θ is the angle between ∇f and u.

→ Intuition

The slope you feel depends on which way you walk:

• θ = 0 — walking along the gradient → maximum ascent (cos = 1).
• θ = 90° — walking perpendicular to gradient → no change (cos = 0). You're tracing a contour line.
• θ = 180° — walking against the gradient → maximum descent (cos = −1).

Why This Cements the Gradient's Special Role

The directional derivative is maximized when u aligns with ∇f. This is the formal proof that the gradient is the direction of steepest ascent.

How this shows up in AI

Adversarial examples (FGSM attack). The Fast Gradient Sign Method crafts an input perturbation that maximally increases the model's loss — moving in the direction of steepest ascent of L with respect to the input x:

xadv = x + ε · sign(∇x L(θ, x, y))

Influence functions and feature attribution (e.g., gradient saliency maps) ask "in which input direction is the model most sensitive?" — a directional derivative question by design.

§ 04

The Hessian Matrix

If the gradient captures slope, the Hessian captures curvature. It's the second derivative — generalized to many dimensions, packaged as a matrix.

Formal Definition

For f: ℝⁿ → ℝ, the Hessian is the n×n matrix of all second partial derivatives:

Hessian Matrix H = ∇²f Hij = ∂²f / ∂xᵢ∂xⱼ

H = ⎡ ∂²f/∂x₁² ∂²f/∂x₁∂x₂ … ∂²f/∂x₁∂xₙ ⎤
    ⎢ ∂²f/∂x₂∂x₁ ∂²f/∂x₂² … ∂²f/∂x₂∂xₙ ⎥
    ⎢ ⋮ ⋮ ⋱ ⋮ ⎥
    ⎣ ∂²f/∂xₙ∂x₁ ∂²f/∂xₙ∂x₂ … ∂²f/∂xₙ² ⎦

Symmetry (Schwarz's theorem): If second partials are continuous, H is symmetric — i.e., ∂²f/∂x∂y = ∂²f/∂y∂x.

Worked Example

For f(x, y) = x²y + 3y²:

∂f/∂x = 2xy, ∂f/∂y = x² + 6y
∂²f/∂x² = 2y, ∂²f/∂y² = 6, ∂²f/∂x∂y = 2x

H(x,y) = ⎡ 2y 2x ⎤
          ⎣ 2x 6 ⎦
→ Intuition

The eigenvalues of the Hessian tell you the curvature along its principal axes:

• All eigenvalues positive → bowl-shaped (local min).
• All eigenvalues negative → upside-down bowl (local max).
• Mixed signs → saddle point.
• Some eigenvalues zero → flat directions; test is inconclusive.

How this shows up in AI

1. Newton's method & second-order optimization. Newton's update is w ← w − H⁻¹∇L — using curvature to take smarter steps than vanilla gradient descent. K-FAC, Shampoo, and Sophia approximate this for deep nets.

2. Loss landscape geometry. The eigenspectrum of the Hessian at a minimum tells us if we're in a sharp or flat valley — flat minima generalize better, a key insight from research on SGD's implicit bias.

3. Saddle points dominate, not local minima. In high-dimensional deep learning losses, almost all critical points are saddles. Understanding when Hessian eigenvalues are mixed-sign explains why SGD's noise helps escape them.

§ 05

Second-Order Conditions

When ∇f = 0, you've found a critical point — but is it a minimum, maximum, or saddle? The Hessian decides.

The Test

At a critical point x* (where ∇f(x*) = 0), examine the Hessian H(x*):

Hessian property Conclusion Picture
Positive definite (all eigenvalues > 0) Strict local minimum Bowl ∪
Negative definite (all eigenvalues < 0) Strict local maximum Inverted bowl ∩
Indefinite (mixed signs) Saddle point Pringle chip 🥨
Positive/negative semidefinite (some zero eigenvalues) Test is inconclusive; need higher-order analysis Flat ridge or trough

2D Quick Test

For f(x, y), define the determinant D = fxx·fyy − (fxy)²:

  • D > 0 and fxx > 0 → local min
  • D > 0 and fxx < 0 → local max
  • D < 0 → saddle point
  • D = 0 → inconclusive

Three faces of a critical point — distinguishable only by the Hessian's eigenvalue signs.

How this shows up in AI

The "saddle point problem" is one of deep learning's central drama-stories. In high-dim loss landscapes, the chance that all eigenvalues happen to be the same sign at a critical point shrinks exponentially with dimension — so saddles vastly outnumber actual minima. SGD's stochastic noise is essentially a saddle-escape mechanism.

§ 06

Taylor Expansion in Multiple Dimensions

Local approximation is everything. Taylor expansion lets you replace a horrifyingly complex function with a friendly polynomial — accurate near a chosen point.

The Formula

Around point a, with displacement Δx = x − a:

Multivariate Taylor (2nd order) f(x) ≈ f(a) + ∇f(a)ᵀ Δx + ½ Δxᵀ H(a) Δx + O(‖Δx‖³)

Three pieces, each linguistically meaningful:

  1. f(a) — the constant value at the anchor point.
  2. ∇f(a)ᵀ Δx — the linear correction (slope × displacement).
  3. ½ Δxᵀ H(a) Δx — the quadratic correction (curvature).

Why It Matters

Almost every optimization algorithm is built on local quadratic approximation:

  • Gradient descent uses the linear (1st-order) Taylor term, picking the descent direction.
  • Newton's method uses the full quadratic (2nd-order) Taylor and jumps directly to the minimum of the local parabola: Δx* = −H⁻¹∇f.
  • Trust region methods trust the quadratic approximation only within a ball of radius δ.
→ Intuition

Near any smooth point, every function looks like a tilted, curved bowl. Taylor expansion is the lens that makes that bowl mathematically explicit.

How this shows up in AI

1. Loss landscape sharpness. The 2nd-order Taylor term ½Δwᵀ H Δw is exactly what "sharpness" measures. Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) optimizers explicitly penalize this term to find flatter minima.

2. Catastrophic forgetting (EWC). Elastic Weight Consolidation uses a Taylor approximation of the loss around old-task weights to add a quadratic penalty: Lnew + λ·(w − w*)ᵀ F (w − w*), where F is the Fisher information (Hessian-like). Pure Taylor in disguise.

3. Neural Tangent Kernels. NTK theory shows wide nets behave like their first-order Taylor expansion around initialization — turning training into linear regression on a fixed feature map.

§ 07

The Jacobian Matrix

Up to now we've considered functions f: ℝⁿ → ℝ — many inputs, one output. The Jacobian generalizes to vector-valued functions f: ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ, which is what every layer of a neural network actually is.

Formal Definition

For f(x) = (f₁(x), f₂(x), …, fm(x)), the Jacobian is the m×n matrix:

Jacobian J = ∂f/∂x Jij = ∂fi / ∂xj

J = ⎡ ∂f₁/∂x₁ ∂f₁/∂x₂ … ∂f₁/∂xₙ ⎤
    ⎢ ∂f₂/∂x₁ ∂f₂/∂x₂ … ∂f₂/∂xₙ ⎥
    ⎢ ⋮ ⋮ ⋱ ⋮ ⎥
    ⎣ ∂fm/∂x₁ ∂fm/∂x₂ … ∂fm/∂xₙ ⎦

Shape Intuition

Function shape Derivative object Shape
f: ℝ → ℝscalar derivative1×1
f: ℝⁿ → ℝgradient ∇f1×n (or n×1)
f: ℝⁿ → ℝᵐJacobian Jm×n
f: ℝⁿ → ℝ (2nd deriv)Hessian Hn×n

Special case: when m = 1, the Jacobian is just the gradient (transposed). The gradient is a special Jacobian.

Multivariable Chain Rule

This is the heart of backprop. If y = f(g(x)), then:

Chain Rule (vector form) ∂y/∂x = Jf(g(x)) · Jg(x)

The Jacobians multiply together — exactly how a neural network's gradients flow backward layer by layer.

How this shows up in AI

1. Every neural network layer is a vector-valued function. Layer i takes activation aᵢ₋₁ (a vector) and produces aᵢ (another vector). Its Jacobian ∂aᵢ/∂aᵢ₋₁ is what gets multiplied in backprop.

2. Vanishing/exploding gradients happen when the product of Jacobian norms across layers shrinks to zero or blows up. Spectral norm regularization, residual connections, and careful initialization all aim to control Jacobian magnitudes.

3. Normalizing flows. Models like RealNVP and Glow rely on the change-of-variables formula, which requires computing log|det J| for invertible transformations. The Jacobian determinant tells you how the model squeezes or stretches probability density.

4. Jacobian-vector products (JVPs) and vector-Jacobian products (VJPs) are the primitives behind PyTorch's autograd and JAX's grad. Forward-mode AD = JVP; reverse-mode AD (used in training) = VJP.

JAX Jacobian computation

import jax import jax.numpy as jnp def f(x): # f: ℝ³ → ℝ² return jnp.array([ x[0]**2 + x[1], x[1] * x[2] ]) x = jnp.array([1.0, 2.0, 3.0]) J = jax.jacrev(f)(x) # J = [[2x₀, 1, 0], # [0, x₂, x₁]] # J = [[2, 1, 0], # [0, 3, 2]]
§ 08

Synthesis: Why This Matters for AI

A single mental model: training a neural network is just multivariable calculus, executed billions of times.

The Master Equation

Boil all of deep learning training to its skeleton:

The Loop θt+1 = θt − η · ∇θ L(θt; D)

Decoded:

  • θ — millions of parameters (weights and biases). High-dim ℝⁿ.
  • L — scalar loss function. ℝⁿ → ℝ.
  • ∇L — gradient: a vector pointing toward higher loss. We negate it to descend.
  • Computation of ∇L — backprop, which is the chain rule applied to a stack of Jacobians (one per layer).
  • Step size choice — implicitly governed by curvature (Hessian); adaptive optimizers approximate this.
  • Generalization quality — partly controlled by the Hessian eigenspectrum at the chosen minimum (sharpness vs flatness).
